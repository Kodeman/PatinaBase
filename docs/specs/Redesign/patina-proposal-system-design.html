04 — Proposal Editor

Write it like a letter, not a form.

The editor IS the proposal. Same Playfair headings. Same Inter body copy. Same strata dividers between sections. When Leah types, she sees exactly what the client will see. Each section is a content block — narrative text, images, product lists, financial tables — flowing continuously. The only chrome: a slim editing toolbar that appears on focus, and section anchors along the right edge for quick jumping.

Design Vision

A living room that honors the 1960s ranch architecture while creating a warm, collected feel for a young family. The south-facing bay window anchors the space with natural light, inviting a reading nook that doubles as a quiet retreat.

We'll work with natural materials throughout — oak, linen, jute — grounding the room in honest textures that only improve with time. The goal isn't a room that looks perfect on day one. It's a room that feels more like home with every passing season.

07 — Revision Workflow

Revise without starting over.

When a client requests changes, the proposal version increments (v1.0 → v1.1). The designer edits the existing document — same layout, same sections. A revision summary captures what changed and why, visible to the client at the top of the updated proposal.

"The proposal is the first piece of design a client receives. If it looks like a spreadsheet, they'll treat you like a vendor. If it reads like a vision, they'll trust you as a partner."
Design Principle — The Proposal Is the First Impression

What makes this different.

The editor is the preview. Leah writes in the same Playfair and Inter typography the client will see. Same strata dividers. Same product layout. When she finishes editing, the proposal is ready — no "preview mode" toggle, no formatting surprises.

Products tell their story. Every selection shows the maker name in italic with their location and founding year. This isn't a shopping list — it's a curated collection with provenance. The anchor pieces are separated from supporting pieces and finishing details, matching the three-tier Curated Shelf architecture.

Engagement gives confidence. Knowing that Sarah viewed the product selections three times in twelve minutes tells Leah more than any status badge. The section-by-section time breakdown reveals exactly where the client's attention lives — and where a follow-up conversation should start.

Signing creates momentum. A signed proposal doesn't just change a status label. It offers to create the project — importing the entire budget, timeline, and product list into Opus. The moment of commitment flows directly into the moment of action. No manual re-entry. No lost context.
